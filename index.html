The central finding — end of 2025
Between 2020 and end-2025, the global hydrogen economy attracted over $750 billion in announced project value and received more than $280 billion in public pledges across the EU, USA, Germany, Australia, Japan, India and South Korea. Of the 1,700+ clean hydrogen projects announced (a 7.5× increase since 2020), only 510 projects (~30%) reached Final Investment Decision (FID), representing $110 billion in committed capital — meaning $640 billion of announced investment has not been confirmed. Only 4% of projects that started construction finished on schedule (PIK/IEA, 2024). In 2025, 4.9 Mtpa of announced capacity was cancelled while only ~1 Mtpa reached FID — a 5:1 attrition ratio. Low-emissions hydrogen production reached the 1 Mtpa milestone in 2025, but still represents less than 1% of global hydrogen output. The fundamental economic problem is unchanged: green hydrogen at $5–9/kg cannot compete with grey hydrogen at $1–1.5/kg — a 4–6× price premium that no subsidy scheme has yet closed. The hydrogen economy as envisioned in 2020–2022 has not materialised. What survives commercially is hydrogen as an industrial chemical feedstock (ammonia, oil refining, steel DRI) — not as a universal energy carrier for heating, transport, or grid storage.

Key Cost Findings
The $1/kg Myth
The US DOE Hydrogen Earthshot program (2021) targeted $1/kg green H₂ by 2030. Current cost is $5–9/kg. Achieving this would require simultaneously: electrolyser costs falling from ~$700/kW to ~$100/kW; electricity costs dropping to ~$10/MWh; and electrolyser utilisation above 90% — conditions that are contradictory (cheap electricity usually means intermittent renewables, implying low utilisation). No credible analyst projects $1/kg before 2040 under optimistic scenarios.
The Gas Price Trap
European natural gas prices spiked in 2021–2022 (to $50–80/MWh TTF), briefly making green hydrogen appear competitive in some scenarios. This was the catalyst for most 2022 project announcements. Gas prices normalised to $10–15/MWh by 2023–2025, immediately collapsing project economics and triggering the cancellation wave. Green hydrogen's viability was always conditional on permanently high gas prices — a basis that proved illusory.
China's Exception
China is on track to reach green hydrogen cost competitiveness by ~2030 due to: ultra-low solar LCOE ($15–25/MWh), domestically manufactured electrolysers at $150–250/kW (50–70% below Western prices), low cost of capital (~4–5% WACC vs 8–12% in Europe/US), and state-directed demand (steel, chemicals). China accounts for 60% of global electrolyser manufacturing capacity and ~50% of FID projects. This is not replicable in high-cost Western contexts without comparable state intervention.
Application Viability — Küfeoğlu (2023) Framework · 20 Business Areas Re-assessed 2025
Küfeoğlu's framework — updated verdict
Küfeoğlu (2023) reviewed 20 prominent hydrogen business opportunities across 64 companies in 18 countries, finding that "many application areas are still struggling to generate a self-reliant revenue stream, falling behind the Valley of Death." The 2025 update is more sobering: only 3 of 20 application areas (ammonia/fertiliser production, oil refining as feedstock, and green steel DRI) show credible commercial pathways without permanent subsidy. Light-vehicle FCEVs, home heating, and power storage are now assessed as non-viable against electrification alternatives. The question is not whether hydrogen has a role — it does as an industrial chemical — but whether it merits the scale of public investment directed at it as an energy carrier.

1. The Learning Curve Indictment — Technology Cost Trajectories 2010–2025
Cost Reduction Index (2010 = 100) — Solar PV · Batteries · Wind · Electrolyser CapEx · Green H₂ LCOH
The single most damning chart in the hydrogen debate. Solar PV has fallen 96% since 2010 (learning rate ~28% per doubling of cumulative capacity). Li-ion batteries fell 90% (learning rate ~18%). Onshore wind fell 55% (learning rate ~8%). Electrolyser CapEx (outside China) fell ~30% in 15 years — a ~4% learning rate, the slowest of any modern energy technology. Green hydrogen LCOH is essentially flat since 2020, and actually higher than 2022 (when gas price normalisation widened the gap with grey H₂). The reason: green H₂ cost depends primarily on electricity price (~55% of LCOH), which has not followed a steady learning curve, and electrolyser cost (~25%), which reduces far too slowly to offset electricity cost volatility. Unlike solar panels or battery cells, electrolysers are large integrated systems with thousands of components — they do not benefit from the same repetitive manufacturing learning as commodity modules.

7. The Public Money Problem & Recommendations
Public Funding Commitments vs Low-Emissions H₂ Production Achieved ($B vs Mtpa)
Return on Public Investment — $B per Mtpa of low-emission H₂ achieved
Five evidence-based recommendations to protect public funds
(1) No subsidy without binding offtake. >80% of cancelled projects had zero binding buyers. Require signed 10-year offtake contracts covering ≥50% of nameplate capacity before any public co-investment. (2) Technology-neutral tendering. In heating (heat pumps 9× cheaper), light vehicles (BEV 4.5× cheaper), grid storage (BESS 1.7× cheaper), direct electrification wins on every metric. Ring-fence hydrogen subsidies to industrial feedstock only. (3) Sunset clauses tied to LCOH milestones. If green H₂ does not reach <$3/kg by 2028 or <$2/kg by 2032, all production subsidies terminate automatically. (4) Independent cost audits. PIK/IEA show developer LCOH estimates are 40–60% too low. All HAR/HPBM/ARRA bids should require third-party validated cost models before award. (5) Annual Parliamentary/Congressional review. The $280k/tonne public cost-per-unit metric should be published annually, alongside cumulative levy commitments, to make the scale of exposure visible to elected representatives and citizens.
Worked Example · Great Britain · DESNZ + Ofgem

The UK has chosen the Regulated Asset Base model
to underwrite a market that doesn't exist.

Britain's approach to hydrogen production, transport, storage and power treats it as if it were a mature regulated utility — water, gas, or electricity. The RAB framework guarantees investors a regulated return regardless of whether anyone consumes the hydrogen. This case study examines the four UK business models, the £2 billion HAR1 commitment, the £241/MWh strike price, and why this architecture is structurally guaranteed to expose GB billpayers to a stranded-asset disaster.

What is RAB? Why is the UK Using It For Hydrogen?
RAB Definition
📚 What is a Regulated Asset Base?
RAB is the financing model used for water companies, gas distribution, electricity transmission, airports and Sizewell C nuclear. The regulator (Ofgem for energy) sets an "allowed revenue" the operator can recover from users — calculated as: asset value × allowed return + depreciation + opex. Crucially, this revenue is paid regardless of how much the asset is used. Investors get a guaranteed regulated return, financed via mandatory user charges.
Why It Works for Utilities
💧 Mature monopolies with universal demand
RAB works for water/gas/electricity because: (1) demand is universal and inelastic (everyone needs water); (2) the network is a natural monopoly (only one set of pipes makes sense); (3) there are no substitutes (you can't replace tap water with electricity); (4) the asset has a 30–60 year predictable life. Volume risk is genuinely low. Investors accept low regulated returns (3–5% real) because the cash flows are bond-like.
Why It Fails for Hydrogen
⚠ A market that doesn't yet exist
Hydrogen has none of these properties: (1) demand is speculative — heat pumps win heating, BEVs win transport, batteries win grid storage; (2) not a natural monopoly — multiple production pathways compete; (3) all major applications have cheaper substitutes; (4) the network may never be needed. Applying RAB to hydrogen means guaranteeing returns to investors for infrastructure that may never have customers — with the bill picked up by gas users via the Gas Shipper Obligation.
Risk Allocation
💸 Who pays when demand fails?
Under DESNZ's design: volume risk sits with the levy (gas users); price risk sits with the levy (top-up to strike price); construction overrun risk shared but with regulated true-up; stranded asset risk sits with the levy. Investors get a 7–9% regulated return on a "Regulatory Capital Value" that grows whether or not the hydrogen is consumed. This is the textbook definition of privatising the upside and socialising the downside.
UK Hydrogen Cost Cascade · Where £241/MWh Goes
HAR1 Strike Price vs Energy Alternatives (£/MWh of useful energy)
HAR1: Where the £2.05B Goes (15-year revenue support)
The £241/MWh strike price means HAR1 hydrogen costs roughly £8.03/kg. Wholesale UK gas in 2025 averages £25–35/MWh (≈£0.90/kg H₂ equivalent for grey). The HAR1 contract structure pays the producer the difference between this strike price and a reference price — meaning the levy makes up ~£200/MWh per unit produced. For context, an ASHP heat pump delivers heat at ~£25–35/MWh after COP, and a gas boiler at ~£55/MWh. Even with full strike-price subsidy, hydrogen domestic heat at HAR1 prices would land at £250–300/MWh at the meter — roughly 10× the cost of heat pump heating.

Risk audit verdict
Of the six categories of risk in any large infrastructure project, five sit with the GB billpayer via the Gas Shipper Obligation. Only technology/performance risk genuinely sits with investors — and even there, regulated returns of 7–9% include a risk premium that effectively prices investors out of any actual exposure. This is not a market mechanism. It is a guaranteed-return scheme where private investors collect regulated profits and the public underwrites every commercial assumption. The Climate Change Committee, the House of Commons Science & Technology Committee, and 32 peer-reviewed scientific studies have concluded that hydrogen will not be used at scale for home heating. Yet the funding architecture forces gas users — disproportionately renters and lower-income households on legacy gas heating — to subsidise a hydrogen economy from which they will never benefit.

Five Recommendations to Protect GB Billpayers
1
No RAB Without Demand
Stop using RAB for unproven markets
RAB is appropriate for natural monopolies with universal, inelastic demand. It is fundamentally unsuited to a technology with no confirmed mass-market application. Defer HTBM/HSBM RAB licensing until binding offtake exists for >50% of regulated capacity for >10 years.
2
Demand-Led Tendering
Reverse the funding logic
Subsidise the offtaker, not the producer. A demand-side contract-for-difference for industrial users (refineries, fertiliser, steel) creates a genuine market signal. Producers compete to serve verified demand. This is how solar PV and offshore wind became cheap.
3
Sunset Clauses
Tie subsidy to cost milestones
Each HAR contract should include sunset triggers: if green H₂ production cost is not below £4/kg by 2030 and £2.50/kg by 2035, subsidy commitments terminate. Permanent levies for permanently uncompetitive technology is fiscal recklessness.
4
Independent Cost Audit
Stop relying on developer LCOH claims
PIK Potsdam research shows developer cost projections are routinely understated by 40–60%. NAO and Ofgem should jointly commission independent LCOH audits before any HAR contract is awarded. Bidders should publish full cost models, not headline strike prices.
5
Annual Parliamentary Review
Make the levy visible
The cumulative cost of HPBM, HTBM, HSBM, H2P, NZHF and CCUS commitments should be reported annually to Parliament with a clear breakdown of: (a) total subsidy committed; (b) hydrogen actually produced; (c) end-uses verified; (d) cost per kg delivered. The current dispersion of these numbers across multiple consultations and impact assessments amounts to fiscal opacity.
6
Industrial Feedstock First
Concentrate effort on the only viable use case
UK hydrogen has one credible commercial pathway: industrial feedstock substitution in existing ammonia, methanol and refining facilities. Concentrate subsidy and policy attention there. Withdraw all support for hydrogen heating, hydrogen vehicles, hydrogen power generation and hydrogen export ambitions. The science consensus on these is settled.
Final assessment of the UK approach
The UK's hydrogen policy architecture — DESNZ-Ofgem RAB regulation, the HPBM/HTBM/HSBM/H2P quadrilogy, the Gas Shipper Obligation — is a textbook example of how to expose public finances to an unproven technology while delivering negligible decarbonisation value. The model treats hydrogen as if it were the next national grid; in reality, it is closer to a chemical commodity with niche industrial applications. Every cancellation listed in the timeline above (H2Teesside, ArcelorMittal Duisburg, Norway parallels, BP exit, H2NE trial) confirms that the underlying economics do not work — and crucially, they do not work even with the level of subsidy already committed. The HAR1 strike price of £241/MWh is not a transitional figure. It is the floor at which the industry can operate today, after several years of cost reduction, with electrolyser CapEx now declining slowly. Public funds are being committed to a technology that competes against electrification on every metric and loses on most. The case for radical retrenchment — concentrating remaining subsidy on industrial feedstock, abandoning the RAB framework for transport and storage, and freezing the GSO until binding offtake exceeds £150M/year of demonstrated demand — is overwhelming.
